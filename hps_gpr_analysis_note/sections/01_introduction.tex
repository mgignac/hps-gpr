\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The Heavy Photon Search (HPS) prompt-resonance program is formulated in the minimal
visible dark-photon model, in which a new broken $U(1)_D$ gauge boson mixes
kinetically with the Standard-Model photon \cite{Holdom1986,EssigEtAl2011}. After
diagonalizing the gauge fields, the interaction terms relevant for the prompt
$A^\prime \rightarrow e^+e^-$ search can be written schematically as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
\supset
-\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}^{\prime}F^{\prime\mu\nu}
-\frac{\eps}{2}F_{\mu\nu}^{\prime}F^{\mu\nu}
\,+\,\frac{1}{2}m_{A^\prime}^{2}A_\mu^\prime A^{\prime\mu}
\,+\,\eps e J_{\mathrm{EM}}^\mu A_\mu^\prime .
\label{eq:dark-photon-lagrangian}
\end{equation}
Here $\eps$ is the kinetic-mixing parameter, $m_{A^\prime}$ is the dark-photon mass,
and $J_{\mathrm{EM}}^\mu$ is the ordinary electromagnetic current. Over the low-mass
region emphasized in this note, the minimal visible model predicts a narrow prompt
dielectron resonance whose natural width is negligible compared with the HPS detector
resolution, so the observed line shape is set entirely by the experimental mass
resolution \cite{HPS2015DarkPhoton,HPS2016PromptLong}. That regime is precisely what
makes the HPS prompt search powerful in the \SIrange{10}{300}{MeV} mass window:
fixed-target electroproduction can probe visibly decaying vectors that would appear as
localized excesses above the smooth trident spectrum, while the relevant coupling range
overlaps both classic dark-sector targets and the long-standing lepton-anomaly
benchmark space.

In the fixed-target approximation relevant for HPS, prompt dark-photon production is
normalized to the radiative trident rate,
\begin{equation}
\sigma(e Z \rightarrow e Z A^\prime)
\simeq
\frac{3\pi \eps^2}{2\alpha_{\mathrm{EM}}}\,
m_{A^\prime}\,
\left.\frac{d\sigma_{\gamma^\ast}}{dm}\right|_{m=m_{A^\prime}},
\label{eq:ap-production}
\end{equation}
so the experimental problem is naturally phrased as the extraction of a narrow signal
yield on top of the smooth prompt $e^+e^-$ spectrum, followed by a conversion of that
yield into a limit or best fit for $\eps^2$
\cite{EssigEtAl2011,HPS2015DarkPhoton,HPS2016PromptLong}. That physics logic is the
through-line of this note: the dark-sector Lagrangian, the minimal prompt $A^\prime$
interpretation, and the radiative-trident normalization are treated as the central
physics objects, while the Gaussian-process-regression (GPR) machinery is the
statistical framework used to realize the search.

\subsection{Published HPS prompt-resonance benchmarks}

The published HPS prompt searches provide the immediate experimental context for the
current GPR analysis. The 2015 engineering-run publication analyzed
\SI{1170}{nb^{-1}} at \SI{1.056}{GeV} over \SIrange{19}{81}{MeV} and reported a lowest
local $p$-value of $1.7\times10^{-3}$ at \SI{37.7}{MeV}; after the look-elsewhere
correction the global $p$-value was 17\%
\cite{HPS2015DarkPhoton}. The 2016 prompt search analyzed the full engineering-run
sample, \SI{10608}{nb^{-1}} at \SI{2.3}{GeV}, over \SIrange{39}{179}{MeV}. Its lowest
local $p$-value was $6.38\times10^{-3}$ at \SI{94}{MeV}, with a global significance of
about $1\,\sigma$, and it propagated mass-resolution and radiative-fraction
systematics explicitly into the final prompt limit \cite{HPS2016PromptLong}.
Figure~\ref{fig:published-hps-context} collects those published HPS prompt benchmarks
in the compact form most relevant for the present GPR note.

\subsection{Complementary prompt-visible constraints}

For internal review, the relevant comparison is not merely which experiments reach the
same mass window, but how their exclusions and favored regions populate the
$(m_{A^\prime},\eps^2)$ plane over roughly \SIrange{10}{300}{MeV}. In that space HPS
occupies a distinct fixed-target niche between very-low-mass meson-decay constraints
and the higher-mass collider reach. Fixed-target electron-beam searches from APEX and
A1/MAMI provide especially relevant low-mass visible benchmarks
\cite{APEX2011,A1MAMI2014}; KLOE/KLOE-2 and BaBar probe complementary visible
production in $e^+e^-$ collisions \cite{KLOE2013,KLOE22015,BaBarDarkPhoton2014}; and
NA48/2 and NA64 constrain, respectively, low-mass meson-decay and visible missing-beam
interpretations that border the same general parameter space
\cite{NA48DarkPhoton2015,NA64X17Visible2021}. Those published contours are the correct
objects for the final review figure because they show exclusion depth, not only mass
coverage.

\subsection{Motivation for the present GPR analysis}

The motivation for the present note is not simply to replace a local polynomial fit
with a different smooth background parameterization. The earlier HPS prompt searches
were built around mass-window-specific background models, window-by-window optimization,
and a resonance-extraction procedure whose numerical behavior depended on the local fit
boundaries. In the current study, each active dataset is described by a single global
GP fit to the invariant-mass spectrum, with the test-mass region removed during
training. That construction provides a mean vector and covariance matrix for the
blinded bins at every mass hypothesis and allows the signal extraction, local
significance, \CLs\ limits, radiative-fraction conversion, and shared-$\eps^2$
combination to be written inside one consistent statistical framework.

This change is especially valuable for the present HPS program because the current GPR
workflow is already being validated over wider scan windows than the published 2015 and
2016 resonance searches. A stable global model therefore matters both for numerical
smoothness and for physics reach: it is what makes it realistic to scan a wider mass
range without losing narrow-resolution sensitivity, to propagate a consistent
radiative-fraction treatment across datasets, and to combine statistically independent
2015, 2016, and 2021 samples in a shared coupling parameter.

\subsection{Scope of this note}

This note documents the current HPS prompt $A^\prime \rightarrow e^+e^-$ GPR analysis
at the level needed for internal physics review. It emphasizes three related physics
inputs: the detector apparatus that defines the mass resolution and acceptance, the
dataset-specific radiative-fraction normalization that converts signal yield into
$\eps^2$, and the profile-likelihood methodology used to quote local significance and
upper limits. The 2015 data are fully unblinded and are used as the most mature
validation sample. The 2016 and 2021 datasets are treated in staged-analysis form, and
the combined 2015+2016 10\%+2021 1\% study is presented as the current shared-coupling
baseline.

The 2019 physics run is mentioned only as part of the detector evolution from the
2015/2016 baseline configuration to the 2019/2021 upgraded configuration. Data were
recorded in 2019 with the upgraded detector, but their dedicated prompt-resonance
analysis is beyond the scope of the present note and will be documented separately.
